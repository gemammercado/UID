Complementary Colors

Complementary colors are pairs of colors which, when combined or mixed, cancel each other out (lose hue) by producing a grayscale color like white or black. When placed next to each other, they create the strongest contrast for those two colors. Complementary colors are opposite each other on the color wheel.

Elina Jermaka, Oranges on Blue Background

This painting uses the complementary pair blue and orange. Notice how the orange pops against the blue background, and the blue looks richer next to the oranges. This high contrast makes both colors appear more vibrant than if they were used alone.

Analogous Colors

Analogous colors are groups of colors that are adjacent to each other on the color wheel, such as red, orange, and red-orange. One color usually dominates, while others enrich the scheme. Analogous schemes often create a sense of harmony and calm.

Claude Monet, Water Lilies (detail)

Monet's Water Lilies series often employs analogous color schemes. This detail shows blues, greens, and violets, colors that sit next to each other on the wheel, creating a tranquil, harmonious effect typical of Impressionism.

Triadic Colors

A triadic color scheme uses colors that are evenly spaced around the color wheel. Triadic schemes tend to be quite vibrant, even if you use pale or unsaturated versions of your hues.
